# Menstrual Cycle and Periods: Comprehensive Reference for AI Analysis

## Purpose

This document is a structured reference on menstruation and the menstrual cycle, designed to be fed into an AI system for analysis, classification, triage support, or question answering.

It is based primarily on reputable medical and public-health sources, especially:

- ACOG (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists)
- NICHD/NIH
- Office on Women's Health (U.S. HHS)
- NHS

This is not a diagnostic tool or medical advice. It should be treated as a knowledge base, not as a substitute for clinician judgment.

## 1. Core Definitions

### Menstruation / period

Menstruation is the shedding of blood and tissue from the uterine lining through the vagina when pregnancy has not occurred.

### Menstrual cycle

The menstrual cycle is the hormone-driven monthly process that prepares the body for pregnancy. It is counted from:

- Day 1 = first day of menstrual bleeding
- End of cycle = day before the next period starts

### Ovulation

Ovulation is the release of an egg from the ovary. In a typical 28-day cycle, ovulation often occurs around the middle of the cycle, but the exact timing varies significantly between people and between cycles.

### Fertile window

Pregnancy is most likely from intercourse in the few days before ovulation and up to the day of ovulation because sperm can survive in the reproductive tract for several days, while the egg survives for a much shorter period.

## 2. What Is Usually Considered Normal

Normal varies by age and life stage. The same person may also have some month-to-month variation.

### Cycle length

- Adults: commonly about 21 to 35 days
- Another widely used normal adult range: 24 to 38 days
- Adolescents: cycles are often more variable, commonly about 21 to 45 days in early years after menarche

### Bleeding duration

- Commonly about 2 to 7 days
- Up to 7 days is generally considered within normal range

### Typical bleeding amount

- Average blood loss is often described as roughly 2 to 3 tablespoons across the whole period
- Actual flow varies widely between individuals

### Menarche and menopause

- First period commonly begins around age 12 on average in the U.S.
- Menarche may normally occur anywhere from about age 8 to 15
- Menopause is defined as 12 consecutive months without a period
- Menopause usually occurs between ages 45 and 55; average age in the U.S. is about 52

## 3. Basic Cycle Physiology: When, How, What

### Menstrual phase

Hormone levels fall, the uterine lining sheds, and bleeding occurs. This starts the cycle.

### Follicular phase

After bleeding begins, follicles develop in the ovaries and estrogen rises. The uterine lining starts rebuilding.

### Ovulation

An egg is released. This does not happen at the same exact day for everyone and may not happen every cycle in some life stages or medical conditions.

### Luteal phase

After ovulation, progesterone rises. If pregnancy does not occur, hormone levels drop and the next period begins.

## 4. Common Symptoms Around a Period

Common symptoms can include:

- Lower abdominal or pelvic cramps
- Lower back pain
- Bloating
- Breast tenderness
- Headache
- Mood changes or irritability
- Food cravings
- Fatigue

These can be normal, but severe pain or heavy bleeding warrants medical review.

## 5. Major Factors That Affect Periods

This section separates factors by strength of evidence and mechanism.

### A. Age and reproductive stage

This is one of the strongest determinants.

#### Puberty / adolescence

- Periods often start irregularly
- It can take years for cycles to become more predictable
- Heavy or variable bleeding can occur in early years because ovulation may not happen regularly

#### Peak reproductive years

- Cycles are often more regular in the 20s and 30s
- Normal variability still exists

#### Perimenopause

- Irregular cycles become more common
- Bleeding may become lighter, heavier, shorter, longer, or more spaced out
- Skipped ovulation becomes more common

#### Pregnancy

- Pregnancy stops ovulation
- Menstrual periods stop during pregnancy
- Bleeding in pregnancy is not a normal menstrual period and should be evaluated clinically

#### Postpartum and breastfeeding

- Periods may not return right away after birth
- Breastfeeding can suppress ovulation, especially with frequent exclusive breastfeeding
- Ovulation can return before the first postpartum period, so pregnancy is still possible

### B. Stress

Strong evidence supports stress as a contributor to cycle changes.

- Chronic or severe stress can disrupt signals between the brain and ovaries
- This can delay ovulation, stop ovulation, or cause missed or irregular periods
- Stress can also worsen PMS-like symptoms and pain perception

### C. Body weight, body fat, and rapid weight change

This is a major factor.

- Significant underweight status or low body fat can suppress ovulation and stop periods
- Eating disorders can cause irregular or absent periods
- Obesity can alter estrogen balance and is associated with irregular, missed, or heavy periods
- Rapid weight loss or weight gain can disrupt normal cycling

### D. Diet and energy availability

Diet affects periods mainly through energy balance, nutritional status, and body composition rather than through single foods.

- Undernutrition can suppress reproduction
- Restrictive eating can contribute to absent or irregular periods
- Poor nutrition can worsen anemia risk if bleeding is heavy
- No strong evidence supports most internet claims that single foods directly start or stop periods

### E. Exercise

Exercise can be helpful overall, but excessive training with low energy intake can disrupt cycles.

- High training load plus low calorie intake can lead to irregular or absent periods
- This is often seen in settings of relative energy deficiency

### F. Sex

Sex does not usually directly control whether a period starts or ends, but it matters indirectly in several important ways.

#### Sex and pregnancy

- Sex without effective contraception can lead to pregnancy
- Pregnancy stops menstrual periods
- Missing a period after sex raises pregnancy as an important possibility

#### Sex and cycle timing

- Sex itself is not a standard biologic driver of cycle length in clinical guidance
- However, cycle changes may appear after sex because of pregnancy, emergency contraception, stress, infections, or coincidence

#### Sex and bleeding

- Bleeding after sex is not considered a normal menstrual pattern
- Causes can include cervical changes, vaginal dryness, infections including STIs, polyps, and more serious causes
- Intermenstrual bleeding or bleeding after sex should be medically assessed

#### Sex during a period

- A person can still have sex during a period
- Pregnancy is less likely during active bleeding in many cycles but is still possible, especially if ovulation occurs early or cycles are short
- STI transmission is still possible

### G. Breastfeeding

Breastfeeding is a major modifier of fertility and period return after childbirth.

- Frequent breastfeeding often delays the return of ovulation and periods
- The timing is highly variable
- Periods may stay absent for months in some people and return much earlier in others
- Once ovulation resumes, pregnancy can happen even before the first visible period

### H. Hormonal birth control and devices

This is one of the strongest non-disease influences on bleeding patterns.

- Combined hormonal methods can make periods lighter and more regular
- Continuous use can reduce the number of periods or stop withdrawal bleeding
- Hormonal IUDs often cause spotting or irregular bleeding at first and may later greatly reduce bleeding or stop it
- The implant can cause unpredictable bleeding: lighter, heavier, longer, infrequent, or absent bleeding
- The injection may eventually stop bleeding in some users
- Copper IUDs can increase bleeding and pain, especially early after insertion
- Emergency contraception can temporarily shift the timing or characteristics of the next bleed

### I. Medical conditions

Several health conditions can affect cycles or bleeding.

#### Ovulatory disorders

- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- Hypothyroidism or other endocrine disorders
- Premature ovarian insufficiency

#### Structural uterine causes

- Fibroids
- Polyps
- Adenomyosis
- Endometriosis may contribute to pain and abnormal bleeding patterns

#### Systemic or metabolic illness

- Diabetes, especially if poorly controlled
- Bleeding disorders
- Significant chronic illness

#### Infection

- Pelvic inflammatory disease
- STIs can contribute to irregular bleeding or bleeding after sex

### J. Medications

- Hormonal contraception can change bleeding patterns
- Blood thinners and aspirin can increase bleeding
- Some hormone therapies can alter cycles or bleeding patterns

## 6. Factors Requested by You: What Matters, What Is Weak, What Is Indirect

### Health

Very important. General health, endocrine health, gynecologic conditions, metabolic disease, nutrition, medication use, and stress all affect periods.

### Age

Very important. Puberty, reproductive years, postpartum state, and perimenopause strongly affect cycle regularity and bleeding pattern.

### Body weight

Very important. Both low and high weight can disrupt periods, especially when changes are rapid or extreme.

### Height

Weak direct effect in routine adult cycle prediction.

- Height is not a major direct clinical determinant of whether a period starts, stops, or becomes irregular
- During growth and puberty, height and overall development may correlate with maturation timing, but adult height is not a standard cycle variable in medical evaluation

### Single vs married

Weak direct biologic effect.

- Relationship status itself is not a recognized physiologic determinant of menstruation
- Any influence is indirect through sexual activity, pregnancy, contraception, stress, sleep, social conditions, or access to care

### Breastfeeding

Strong effect postpartum. It can delay ovulation and return of periods.

### Health issue / illness

Strong effect depending on condition. Endocrine, gynecologic, metabolic, nutritional, hematologic, and infectious conditions all matter.

## 7. Important Things AI Should Not Oversimplify

### Periods are not always monthly

Many people use "monthly period" casually, but medically the cycle can be normal over a range and does not need to be exactly 28 days.

### A missed period is not explained by one factor by default

The most important first exclusion after sex is pregnancy. After that, consider stress, weight change, illness, endocrine issues, medications, breastfeeding, and life stage.

### Bleeding is not always a period

Bleeding may instead be:

- Implantation-related bleeding
- Breakthrough bleeding from contraception
- Bleeding after sex
- Infection-related bleeding
- Miscarriage-related bleeding
- Perimenopausal bleeding changes
- Pathologic abnormal uterine bleeding

### Regular bleeding does not guarantee ovulation

Some irregular cycles are ovulatory, some are not. Some bleeding can occur without normal ovulation.

### No single factor should be used alone

Accurate interpretation usually requires combining:

- Age/life stage
- Pregnancy possibility
- Breastfeeding/postpartum status
- Contraception use
- Cycle history
- Bleeding pattern
- Weight change
- Stress
- Symptoms
- Medical history

## 8. Warning Features That Need Medical Review

These are clinically important red flags from major guidance.

- No period for 3 months or more when not pregnant or breastfeeding
- Bleeding after sex
- Bleeding between periods
- Very heavy bleeding, such as soaking a pad or tampon every hour for more than 2 hours
- Bleeding lasting more than 7 or 8 days
- New irregular periods after previously normal cycles
- Severe pain, fainting, chest pain, shortness of breath, or dizziness with heavy bleeding
- Bleeding after menopause
- Pregnancy with bleeding, especially with pain

## 9. AI Analysis Framework

If you want an AI to analyze menstrual health data, these are the most useful structured inputs.

### Core demographic and life-stage variables

- Age
- Age at menarche
- Menopausal status
- Pregnancy status
- Postpartum status
- Breastfeeding status

### Cycle history variables

- Average cycle length
- Minimum cycle length
- Maximum cycle length
- Variation month to month
- Duration of bleeding
- Estimated flow severity
- Presence of clots
- Spotting between periods
- Bleeding after sex
- Date of last menstrual period
- Number of missed periods

### Symptom variables

- Pain severity
- PMS symptoms
- Headache
- Breast tenderness
- Bloating
- Mood changes
- Fatigue
- Pelvic pain outside period
- Fever
- Vaginal discharge changes

### Reproductive and sexual variables

- Sexually active: yes/no
- Unprotected sex: yes/no
- Date of last sex
- Pregnancy test result
- Emergency contraception use
- Contraception method
- STI risk or diagnosis

### Health variables

- Weight
- Recent weight change
- Height
- BMI if needed
- Eating disorder history
- Exercise level
- Stress level
- Sleep disruption
- Thyroid disease
- PCOS
- Diabetes
- Bleeding disorder
- Fibroids/polyps/endometriosis/adenomyosis
- Current medications

### Output labels an AI may predict

- Likely normal variation
- Possible pregnancy-related change
- Possible breastfeeding/postpartum suppression
- Possible stress-related irregularity
- Possible weight/nutrition/exercise-related irregularity
- Possible contraception-related bleeding change
- Possible endocrine disorder
- Possible structural uterine cause
- Possible infection or STI-related bleeding
- Needs urgent medical review

## 11. Suggested AI Reasoning Rules

These are safe high-level heuristics, not diagnosis rules.

1. First assess pregnancy possibility if a period is late and sex has occurred.
2. Treat breastfeeding/postpartum status as a major explanation for absent or delayed periods, but do not assume infertility.
3. Treat hormonal contraception as a major explanation for spotting, absent bleeding, or changed bleeding.
4. Treat severe stress, rapid weight change, eating disorder, or very intense exercise as strong causes of irregular or absent periods.
5. Treat PCOS, thyroid disease, diabetes, fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis, endometriosis, and bleeding disorders as important medical explanations.
6. Treat bleeding after sex, bleeding between periods, very heavy bleeding, bleeding after menopause, or pregnancy with bleeding as escalation signals.
7. Do not assign strong direct causal value to marital status or adult height.

## 12. Limits and Cautions for AI Use

- Menstrual patterns vary widely and some "abnormal" patterns may still be benign.
- A period tracker cannot diagnose ovulation with certainty by dates alone.
- Reported bleeding may not represent menstruation.
- Self-reported cycle data may be incomplete or inaccurate.
- Hormonal contraception can make "period-like" bleeding different from a natural menstrual cycle.
- Postpartum and perimenopausal patterns are especially variable.

## 13. Practical Summary

The strongest drivers of menstrual timing and pattern are:

- Age and reproductive stage
- Pregnancy status
- Breastfeeding/postpartum state
- Hormonal contraception
- Stress
- Body weight and recent weight change
- Nutrition and exercise balance
- Endocrine and gynecologic conditions

The weakest direct factors from your list are:

- Marital status
- Adult height

These may still matter indirectly through pregnancy, sex, social context, or health access, but they are not primary physiologic drivers in standard clinical guidance.

## 15. Inference Notes

These points are evidence-based in overall direction, but phrased as synthesis rather than direct statements from one single source:

- Sex usually affects periods indirectly rather than directly, mainly through pregnancy, contraception, infection, stress, or emergency contraception.
- Diet mostly affects menstruation through energy balance, body weight, body fat, and nutritional status rather than individual foods.
- Marital status and adult height are weak direct predictors of menstrual timing or bleeding pattern.
